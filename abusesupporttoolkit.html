üîç What is Abuse?

×
Abuse is a pattern of behaviour used to gain and maintain control over another person.

It doesn't always leave bruises. It doesn't always happen loudly. But its effects can be devastating ‚Äî emotionally, physically, and psychologically.

Abuse can be:

  • Chronic or one-time
  • Subtle or obvious
  • Overt or hidden in "jokes," silence, or control

People often suffer in silence out of fear, shame, love, trauma bonds, or lack of safe support.

üíî Emotional/Psychological Abuse

×
Often overlooked because there are no physical marks ‚Äî but emotional abuse is deeply damaging.

What it looks like:

  • Gaslighting ("That never happened" / "You're too sensitive")
  • Guilt-tripping or emotional blackmail
  • Withholding affection as punishment
  • Intimidating stares, slamming doors
  • Threats of self-harm to manipulate

Long-term effects:

  • Anxiety, self-doubt, panic attacks
  • Difficulty trusting others
  • People-pleasing or avoiding conflict
  • Trouble identifying your own feelings

üí™ Physical Abuse

×
The most recognized form of abuse, but not the only one.

What it looks like:

  • Grabbing, shaking, punching, slapping
  • Forcefully restraining or dragging
  • Blocking someone from leaving a room
  • Hurting pets or destroying objects

It's not just bruises ‚Äî also includes:

  • Being denied medical care
  • Sleep deprivation
  • Forced substance use

üí∏ Financial/Economic Abuse

×
Designed to make someone dependent, unable to leave.

What it looks like:

  • Monitoring or controlling your bank accounts
  • "Giving" money as manipulation or control
  • Forcing financial decisions without consent
  • Refusing to pay child support or bills intentionally
Why it matters:
Many survivors stay because they simply can't afford to leave.

üíî Sexual Abuse/Assault

×
Sexual abuse can happen inside or outside relationships. Consent must be ongoing, enthusiastic, and mutual.

What it looks like:

  • Being touched without consent
  • Being pressured or guilted into sex
  • Tampering with birth control ("stealthing")
  • Sexual humiliation or criticism
Important:
Sexual abuse can happen even in marriage or relationships. "No" is always valid.

üåê Digital/Technological Abuse

×
As technology evolves, so do abusive tactics.

What it looks like:

  • Demanding access to passwords
  • Monitoring calls/texts/socials
  • Using GPS tracking without consent
  • Sextortion ‚Äî threatening to leak explicit content
  • Impersonating you online

Hidden signs:

  • Always needing to reply immediately
  • Being punished for what you post or like
  • Having apps deleted or accounts "snooped on"

üë§ Neglect

×
Especially common in child, elder, and disability abuse ‚Äî it's when care is intentionally withheld.

What it looks like:

  • Not feeding, cleaning, or giving water/medication
  • Leaving vulnerable people alone for long periods
  • Failing to meet emotional needs (love, attention)
Neglect is abuse, even if it's not violent.

üö© Red Flags People Miss

×
Sometimes abuse is masked as:
  • "Protectiveness" that isolates you
  • "Jealousy" that restricts you
  • "Helping" with finances that becomes control
  • Sarcasm that becomes public humiliation
  • "Tough love" that's actually emotional abuse
Trust your instincts: If something feels wrong, it probably is. Your feelings are valid.

üß† Understanding Trauma & Abuse Cycles

×
Abuse often follows a cycle:
  1. Tension Building: walking on eggshells
  2. Incident: outburst or harm
  3. Reconciliation: apologies, love bombing
  4. Calm: "honeymoon" phase

This can make victims hopeful things will change ‚Äî but often, the cycle repeats.

Trauma bonding is real. It's not your fault if you stayed. It's not your fault at all.

üí¨ Survivor Truths

×
  • Abuse is never your fault
  • You do not need bruises to prove pain
  • Leaving is often the most dangerous time ‚Äî safety planning is key
  • Healing is not linear ‚Äî some days are harder than others
  • You are not "too broken" to heal ‚Äî you're brave for surviving

"I survived because the fire inside me burned brighter than the fire around me."

‚Äî Joshua Graham

"My worth is not measured by someone else's inability to see it."

"I am allowed to take up space. I am allowed to say no. I am allowed to leave."

üß≠ How to Help a Loved One

×
  • Listen without judgment or interruption
  • Avoid saying "Why don't you just leave?" (It's more complicated than that)
  • Offer to go with them to appointments, court, or therapy
  • Help them create a safety plan
  • Let them make their own choices ‚Äî reclaiming power is part of healing
Remember: Your role is to support, not to rescue. Empowerment comes from within.

üîê Safety Planning

If you're planning to leave or escape:
  • Pack an emergency bag (ID, keys, money, meds, copies of important documents)
  • Use incognito mode on browsers, delete history
  • Tell a trusted friend your plan and check-in times
  • Know where you'll go: shelter, friend's house, or even a safe public place
  • Avoid confrontation when leaving if possible ‚Äî safety first
