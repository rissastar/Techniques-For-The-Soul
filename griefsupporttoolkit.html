Understanding Grief

Grief is a natural, complex response to loss, affecting emotional, physical, and mental health. It can come in waves, sometimes unexpectedly, and there is no ‚Äúright‚Äù way to grieve.

While grief often feels overwhelming, it is also a sign of love and connection. By understanding its process, you can gently navigate your feelings and find your own path toward healing.

Explore the common stages of grief

While experiences vary, many people go through these stages:

  • Denial: Struggling to accept the reality of loss, feeling numb or in shock.
  • Anger: Experiencing frustration, bitterness, or helplessness about what happened.
  • Bargaining: Searching for ways to reverse or undo the loss, often with "what if" thoughts.
  • Depression: Deep sadness, withdrawal, and processing the reality of the loss.
  • Acceptance: Coming to terms with the loss and finding a new normal.

Remember: these stages are not linear. You may revisit some stages many times, or experience others uniquely.

Coping Strategies

When grief feels heavy, these gentle methods can provide comfort and support:

  • Mindful breathing: Try slow, deep breaths ‚Äî inhale for 4 seconds, hold for 4, exhale for 6.
  • Allow expression: Cry if you need to, write letters to your loved one, or speak your feelings aloud.
  • Connect: Reach out to trusted friends, family, or support groups. You are not alone.
  • Self-care routines: Set small goals each day, like drinking water, walking outside, or resting.
  • Nature & movement: Gentle walks, yoga, or sitting in a peaceful place can soothe your mind.
  • Creative outlets: Draw, paint, or craft memories and feelings as a form of healing.

Self-Care Reminders

  • Be patient and gentle with yourself; healing takes time.
  • Rest and allow your body to recover when needed.
  • Maintain nourishing meals, even if small portions feel easier.
  • Find quiet moments to breathe, meditate, or simply be.
  • Accept help and support from others when offered.
